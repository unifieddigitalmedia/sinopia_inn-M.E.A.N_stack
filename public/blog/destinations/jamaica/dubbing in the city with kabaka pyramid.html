DUBBING IN THE CITY WITH KABAKA PYRAMID

Soon after arriving on the direct British Airways flight from London, England’s Gatwick Airport, into Kingston’s Norman International Airport, I was out in the sun and on my way. A first as Im used to flying via Miami or New York with British Airways into the popoluar tourist arrival airport of Sangster's in Montego Bay. Nonetheless I was pleasantly surprised by the combination of the sun beating down and the cool breeze blowing over the Caribbean Sea as I rattled along the empty coast road into the city. Interesting fact: Jamaica has more churches per square kilometre than any other country in the world.

During my visit through the months of October and November Jamaica’s rich history and local culture shone bright as the islands National Heroes' Day was celebrated on the third Mon in October. Every visitor to the island can expect to taste the best in Jamaican cooking as chefs from every corner of the island came out to showcase their culinary skills at the island’s annual Food and Drink Festival and Restaurant Week. Other notable dates to travel are on public holidays. New Year's Day; Ash Wednesday; Good Friday; Easter Monday; Labour Day which falls on May 23rd; Independence Day which falls on the first Mon in August; Christmas and Boxing Day.

But what to expect? Definitely rum, roots and reggae - all of the things Jamaica is known for but while I waited for the annual Restaurant Week to kick off, I was able to discover so much more throughout the city. New bands, music venues, festivals, events and food. A few spots in Kingston that stood out were Devon House the former governor's residence on Hope Road which has a pleasingly elegant restaurant where you can eat well. Recbones The Blues Cafe where the politicians and industry shakers on Braemar Avenue, a relaxing spot that regualr features a live band and local acts. The Cabin On The Sea in Port Royal is also nice with tables set on a jetty over the sea and for a vegartaian alternative I tried Just Salads. Tucked in the back of an old bungalow, nothing fancy but the prices are extraordinarily good, and the salads are consistently tantalizing.



picture courtesy of Matthew Henry of caribbean-beat.com

A another great place to go for an vegaratian alternative is Dub Club, not only a resturant but an amazing cultural platform for emerging artists hosted by Gabriele Selassie, who recalls that after speaking with his good friend and international reggae superstar Luciano, he decided to launch Dub Club to deliver a platform for reggae music all the year round - and not just at the grand staged events of Reggae Sumfest and Rebel Salute.



picture courtesy of Matthew Henry of caribbean-beat.com

Entrance is free throughout the week from 12:00 to 10:00pm except on Sunday when Gabriele Selassie invites artists to perform or wows the crowd with a all night long juggling session of rocking reggae beats in the cool hills above Kingston overlooking the amazing skyline. Here some of my oldest friends have featured as they have journeyed on to international reggae stardom. During this trip to Jamaica I was lucky enough to sit down with Kabaka Pyramid who shared his most recent travel experience with me.
