FINDING SINOPIA INN IS EASY

Stepping out of Sinopia Inn into its natural environment was great. The cool green hills formed a dramatic backdrop to the historic architecture of the parish’s residential homes and town buildings. The vast amount of greenery accelerated by the rain cooled the parish in the day time and made my trip even more attractive.

Not only did this cool scenic route remind me of the challenge of the air pollution we’re facing in our major cities but it also left me appreciative of this breath of fresh air - and also of the tremendous wealth to be gained by sustainable and self-sufficient living. All food produce served at Sinopia Inn was sourced locally, offering fresh and seasonal dishes that tasted great. I have no doubt that this was due to Portland’s soil and heavy rains that were well suited to organic cultivation.


So I asked for a tour of the farm hoping to get a few tip / tricks to take back to my urban dwelling and was astounded to see 10 acres of farm land filled with an array of fruits and ground provisions plus a few chickens. Immediately I knew that Sinopia Inn definitely showed signs of future promise. It may seem a long way off but the community benefits may drive sustainable farming with a little help.


Check out the banana’s timeline in Jamaica and some fun things you can do with it
  • 1835 - GROS Michel variety of bona first planted in St Andrew, quickly spread to parishes with more fertile soils and higher rainfall levels, especially in north and east of the island whee sugar lands were abandoned.
  • 1912 - Claude Mckay referred to it in his poem “KING BANANA”
  • 1860 - small American schooners began to carry bananas as well as pineapples , coconuts, oranges and yam from northern Jamaican ports, especially Port Antonio , to New York and Boston
  • 1870 - the banana emerged as a profitable economy in Jamaicas languishing export economy
  • 1886 - Jamaica Gleaner reported that the yellow or Martinique banana had become “known in the United States as the Jamaican banana to distinguish it from the large red banana formerly exported in large quantities from Cuba. The Cuban banana has now been almost entirely replaced by the brighter and more attractive as well as luscious fruit from Jamaica.
  • 1890 - Banana figs were made in factories for export
  • 1893 - Sullivan offered twelve recipes in her section on puddings and preserves
  • 1897 - Direct shipment to the United Kingdom commenced
  • 1902/03 - Jamaica shipped 13 million bunches of bands to the United States and 1 million to the United Kingdom. United Fruit Company in the United States and Elders and Fyffes in the United Kingdom emerged to control both production and trade.
  • 1910 - United Fruit absorbed Elders and Fyffes
  • 1912 - Banana Food Manufacturing Company was established din Montego Bay to turn surplus and rejected stems into bona flour for export as well as local consumption
  • 1917 - the boiled green banana was being called a hard time food, but it was cheap one and one of the staples of the poor classes diet .
  • 1940 - Bananas were given away in large quantities
  • 1942 - the people of Jamaica developed a habit of eating bananas and starting to buy them
  • 1910 - Gros Michel was struck around 1910 by Panama disease and replaced by the Cavendish variety
  • 1924 - Jamaica established one of the earliest banana - breeding programmes and produced many more varities.
  • 1960 - Banana chips were first produced commercially
  • 1964 - Native Food Packers was founded used surplus bands to develop what became a fashionable snack under the hippies brand.
  • 1965 - Produced peaked at about 200,000 tonnes and then fell to 25,000 tonnes by 2005
  • 1965 - Culinary Arts Competition associated with the Jamaica Festival highlighted the banana
  • 2000 - Native Food Frackers produced bona and plantain chips as well as cheese curls, fruit pops, onion bits , nuts and popcorn
